## Chapter 1: The State of Venture Capital

The venture capital market from 2024 to 2026 isn't dead—it's undergoing a fundamental restructuring. Capital is concentrating into fewer, larger winners while the middle market hollows out. Understanding this bifurcation is essential for everyone in the ecosystem.

The numbers tell a stark story. Total venture capital deployed dropped from $330 billion at the 2021 peak to roughly $170 billion by 2024—a decline of nearly fifty percent. The number of deals fell by a similar proportion, from over 17,000 to around 9,500. Median valuations at every stage contracted: seed valuations dropped twenty percent, Series A fell by about eleven percent, and Series B valuations declined by a third. Perhaps most telling, the number of funds raised collapsed by sixty-four percent, from over 1,100 to approximately 400.

At the same time, the frequency of down rounds quadrupled, going from five percent of all deals to twenty percent. This isn't a minor correction—it's a fundamental repricing of the entire asset class.

The Limited Partner liquidity crisis sits at the heart of these changes. Since 2022, LPs have been called for over $400 billion in capital contributions, but they've received only about $200 billion in distributions. That $200 billion gap explains why so many institutional investors have pulled back from new fund commitments. They simply can't afford to keep writing checks when their existing portfolio isn't generating returns.

The market is splitting into two distinct worlds. On one side are the mega-funds—vehicles with a billion dollars or more under management. These funds capture roughly eighty percent of LP dollars, generate lower expected returns in the twelve to fifteen percent range, and operate increasingly like private equity firms. They focus on later-stage deals with larger check sizes, and their competitive advantage comes from brand recognition, platform services, and sheer capital availability.

On the other side are emerging managers with sub-$100 million funds. They're fighting for the remaining twenty percent of LP capital but often generate higher potential returns above twenty percent. These managers make concentrated bets, take true venture risk, and focus on pre-seed and seed investments. Their edge comes from hustle, unique access, and the ability to take meaningful ownership positions.

The middle—funds between $100 million and $500 million—is disappearing. These managers are too large to generate emerging manager returns but too small to compete with mega-fund resources. They're struggling to define their identity in the new landscape.

What does this mean for each player in the ecosystem?

If you're a founder, raise capital when you can, not when you must. The Series A gap is real—plan for at least twenty-four months of runway. Consider emerging managers seriously; they'll give you more attention and make faster decisions. And don't optimize for the highest valuation; optimize for the best partner.

If you're a Limited Partner, diversify across fund sizes. Emerging managers consistently outperform at early stages. Accept that distributions will be delayed and resist the urge to panic-sell on secondary markets. Maintain relationships with first-time fund managers—they remember who backed them when it mattered. And adjust your expectations: plan for twelve to fifteen year fund cycles, not ten.

If you're a General Partner, right-size your fund to match realistic exit markets. A $50 million fund needs a $500 million exit to be successful; a $1 billion fund needs multiple $5 billion outcomes. Focus on ownership percentage rather than check size. And build LP relationships during downturns when everyone else retreats—that's when lasting partnerships are forged.

The recovery is coming. By 2025, we should see early signs: select IPOs returning, M&A activity picking up, and AI driving a new investment cycle. By 2026, the IPO window should open wider, LP distributions should increase, and fund formation should rebound. Full normalization likely won't arrive until 2027 or 2028, but the groundwork is being laid now.

## Chapter 2: The J-Curve Is Gone (For Now)

The traditional venture fund J-curve—where fund value declines in the early years before rising sharply as investments mature—has effectively flattened. Funds raised in 2021 and 2022 marked up their portfolios aggressively during the boom, then watched those values go flat as companies grew into their inflated valuations without new exit opportunities.

Historically, fund value would dip ten to twenty percent in years one and two as management fees were drawn and early investments hadn't yet appreciated. Then, between years five and seven, successful exits would drive returns to two or three times committed capital. The current pattern looks nothing like this. Instead, funds spiked to 1.5x or 2x paper value by year two, then flatlined to year five and beyond.

The math of what happened is straightforward. In 2021, venture capital deployed $330 billion—roughly double a normal year. Valuations soared to two to three standard deviations above historical means. Companies raised rounds every six to nine months, generating rapid paper gains. Exit activity was strong, which encouraged even more aggressive deployment.

Then everything reversed. Interest rates went from zero to five percent, exploding the cost of capital. Public technology markets dropped thirty to fifty percent. The IPO window slammed shut. Regulatory scrutiny froze M&A activity. And critically, General Partners were reluctant to mark down their portfolios even as public comparables crashed.

The result: paper gains from 2021 are stuck. There are no exits to realize that value. Companies are growing into their valuations rather than beyond them.

Consider a hypothetical $100 million fund raised in 2021. In year one, the manager deployed $30 million across ten companies at an average entry valuation of $20 million. Quick markups pushed the portfolio value to $60 million—a 2x TVPI. LP reports celebrated "great momentum."

In year two, another $25 million was deployed, but markets crashed. Marks held steady because there were no new rounds to reset prices. TVPI dipped slightly to 1.8x. The LP letter noted "markets are tough, but our portfolio remains resilient."

By year three, deployment slowed to $15 million. Some markdowns were finally taken. Two portfolio companies raised at lower valuations. TVPI fell to 1.4x. The GP talked about "right-sizing to reality."

In year four, just $10 million was deployed, mostly reserves. Portfolio companies were growing twenty to thirty percent annually—respectable, but nothing like the hundred percent growth of the boom times. No markups because there were no new rounds at higher prices. TVPI dropped to 1.3x, with DPI at just 0.1x from one small exit.

By year five, TVPI has recovered slightly to 1.4x, but DPI remains at only 0.2x. LPs are starting to ask: "When do we see real returns?"

The gap between expectations and reality is substantial. A 2021 vintage fund would normally expect 2x or better TVPI and 0.5x or higher DPI by year five. Instead, they're delivering 1.4x TVPI and 0.2x DPI.

What should different players take from this?

Limited Partners shouldn't panic-sell on secondary markets at forty percent or greater discounts. The 2021 vintage funds will take ten to twelve years to mature, not seven to eight. Evaluate GPs on their process and decision-making, not just current marks. The recovery is coming—the timing is uncertain, but the direction is not.

General Partners must be honest about marks. Credibility matters more than short-term TVPI maintenance. Communicate proactively with LPs about extended timelines. Focus on helping portfolio companies reach profitability, which gives them control over their own destiny.

Founders should understand that their investors are under significant pressure. Focus on efficient growth rather than growth at all costs. Profitability is the path to controlling your destiny. And remember: the best time to raise is always before you absolutely need to.

## Chapter 3: Why Vertical AI Wins

The biggest opportunity in artificial intelligence isn't building foundation models. That game belongs to Big Tech companies with their $50 billion-plus annual R&D budgets. The real opportunity lies in applying AI to specific industries with domain expertise, proprietary data, and deep workflow integration.

Vertical AI companies build moats that horizontal players simply cannot replicate.

The landscape already demonstrates this thesis. More than 140 vertical AI startups have reached significant scale, with a combined valuation exceeding $120 billion. Healthcare and biotech lead with eighteen companies worth $24.5 billion combined. Financial services follows with fifteen companies worth $19.2 billion. Legal tech has produced twelve companies worth nearly $9 billion. Manufacturing, logistics, real estate, construction, education, agriculture, energy, and retail each have meaningful representation.

The average valuation across these vertical AI companies exceeds $850 million, proving that focused, domain-specific approaches can build substantial businesses.

The comparison between horizontal and vertical AI is instructive. Horizontal AI companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta chase massive total addressable markets exceeding $500 billion. But they face five to ten competitors, each backed by $100 billion or more. They need $10 billion or more just to compete on model development. Their moat comes from scale, compute infrastructure, and research talent. And they face margin pressure from commoditization as models become increasingly interchangeable.

Vertical AI companies target smaller markets—perhaps $5 billion to $50 billion per vertical—but face far less competition. They typically need $50 million to $200 million to build a dominant position. Their moat comes from domain expertise, proprietary data, and workflow integration. They can command premium pricing for specialized value. And startups with genuine industry expertise can win.

The moat stack in vertical AI builds in layers, each more defensible than the last.

At the foundation is switching costs: data lock-in, training investments, and integration complexity. This provides meaningful but not insurmountable protection.

Above that sits customer trust. Regulated industries require proof before adoption—FDA clearances, SOC2 compliance, HIPAA certification, case studies. Building this trust takes time and sustained investment.

The third layer is domain expertise. Teams that truly understand the industry's real problems have a genuine advantage. This can be hired for, but building the right culture takes time.

Workflow integration forms the fourth layer. Products embedded in daily operations, not used as separate tools, are extremely sticky. Think AI that lives inside electronic health records, CAD software, or ERP systems.

At the top sits proprietary data—industry-specific training data that improves your models in ways competitors cannot easily replicate. Claims data, legal precedents, manufacturing logs: these take years to accumulate.

Notice what's not on this list: the foundation model itself. That's increasingly commodity. The value is in everything built on top.

When evaluating vertical AI investments, five factors matter most.

Data moat deserves twenty-five percent weight. Ask whether the company has proprietary data others can't easily obtain, and how long it would take a competitor to replicate it.

Workflow depth also deserves twenty-five percent weight. Is this the system of record or a nice-to-have feature? How often do users interact with the product?

Domain team warrants twenty percent weight. Did the founders work in this industry? Do they have credibility with actual buyers?

Margin structure gets fifteen percent weight. Are these software margins above seventy percent, or is there services drag pulling margins below fifty percent?

Market timing receives the final fifteen percent. Is the industry ready for AI? Are budgets allocated? Who is the actual buyer?

A weighted score above 4.0 suggests a strong investment candidate. Between 3.5 and 4.0, the opportunity is worth serious diligence. Between 3.0 and 3.5, there are concerns to address. Below 3.0, pass.

Harvey, the legal AI company, exemplifies what good looks like. Founded in 2022, the company has raised over $200 million and achieved a valuation exceeding $1.5 billion by 2024.

On data moat, Harvey scores five out of five. They've built partnerships with major law firms to train on legal documents, case outcomes, precedents, and contract language. The system improves with every interaction.

On workflow integration, they score four out of five. They've embedded themselves into existing legal workflows—drafting, research, and review are all integrated into how associates work daily.

On domain team, another five out of five. The founder was both a practicing litigator and holds a machine learning PhD. Team members include former Big Law partners who have instant credibility with buyers.

On margin structure, four out of five. It's a software model with a usage component, achieving seventy percent plus gross margins through enterprise contracts.

On market timing, five out of five. Law firms face billing pressure. Clients demand efficiency. AI is obviously applicable to text-heavy legal work.

The weighted score: 4.6 out of 5—an exceptional vertical AI company.

Watch out for warning signs. "We use AI for this industry" often means they're just wrapping ChatGPT. Teams without industry experience struggle to earn buyer trust. Solutions looking for problems rarely find them. Companies competing directly with horizontal features from Microsoft or Google face long odds. Services-heavy delivery models drag down margins. And if there's no clear buyer or budget, there's no business.

The action items are clear. Founders should pick one vertical and go absurdly deep—you can't out-generalize OpenAI. Build on proprietary data from day one; it's your moat. Investors should score every vertical AI deal against this framework and always ask "What data do you have that OpenAI doesn't?" Enterprises should buy vertical AI where it exists. You cannot build this internally fast enough to matter.

## Chapter 4: Enterprise AI Adoption

Enterprise AI adoption is slow not because the technology doesn't work. The tools are ready. The users are ready. The problem is institutional: misaligned incentives across executives, middle managers, and individual contributors create bottlenecks that technology alone cannot solve.

The data reveals a troubling pattern. Among Fortune 500 companies, about forty percent claim to have deployed AI in production. But only eighteen percent report that AI generates measurable ROI. In the mid-market, twenty percent claim deployment but just eight percent see returns. For SMBs, the numbers are ten percent and four percent respectively.

The gap is striking: deployment claims far exceed actual value creation. Most "deployments" are pilots that never scale.

This happens because of a three-layer incentive problem.

At the C-suite level, executives say AI is transformational and demand immediate adoption. What they actually want is innovation optics and strategic positioning. They approve budgets and demand ROI metrics. Their incentive is to look visionary and avoid being disrupted. So they champion pilots but delegate actual implementation, rarely staying involved long enough to see them through.

The real bottleneck sits with middle management. Middle managers say they need to form a committee to evaluate options. What they actually want is to not get fired and to protect their teams. They slow-roll decisions and request more studies. Their incentive is to maintain the status quo and reduce personal risk. So they create process overhead and delay deployment, letting pilots die quietly.

Individual contributors say "Why should I train my replacement?" What they want is job security and fair compensation. They adopt AI minimally and offer quiet resistance. Their incentive is to protect their role; they have no upside for improving efficiency. So they use AI privately but hide productivity gains, never letting the organization capture the full benefit.

The math from an individual contributor's perspective is brutal. What are the benefits of adopting AI visibly? You complete tasks faster, but you don't get a raise. You produce more output per hour, but your salary stays the same. Your work becomes measurable, which means more scrutiny. Your skills get documented, making you easier to replace. Net benefit: zero or negative.

What are the risks? AI digitizes your tacit knowledge, meaning anyone can do your job. It exposes performance gaps, making it harder to hide. It automates your workflows, increasing layoff risk. It documents your methods, eliminating your secret sauce. Net risk: significant.

The rational decision, from a purely self-interested perspective, is minimal visible adoption, private use for personal gain, and resistance to enterprise-wide rollout.

Middle managers face a lose-lose situation. If AI succeeds, their teams shrink and their power decreases. Work automates and their role gets questioned. Individual contributors become more productive, requiring fewer people to manage. The result: "Why do we need you?"

If AI fails, they championed it and get blamed. Budget was spent and they're accountable. Disruption occurred and they're responsible. The result: career damage.

The rational response is to not champion anything, request more "evaluation," create committees and processes, never actually decide, and let pilots die quietly.

Enterprises can assess their AI readiness across six factors. Is there a named executive sponsor with budget authority, or just a committee? Are success metrics specific KPIs or vague goals like "improve efficiency"? Is data clean and accessible, or messy and locked in silos? Does a training and change management plan exist, or is the hope that people will figure it out? Is compensation tied to adoption, or is there just recognition? Is pilot scope narrow and measurable, or broad and ambitious?

High scores on these factors suggest the organization can proceed with confidence. Low scores indicate significant foundational work is required before AI can succeed.

The build versus buy decision should usually favor buying. Start with a simple question: does AI provide competitive differentiation in your core product? If no, buy immediately and focus on your actual business. If yes, ask whether AI is your core business. If it's not, ask whether you have proprietary data that creates advantage. Without that proprietary data, you should still buy—you can't replicate what specialized vendors have accumulated.

The reality is that ninety percent or more of enterprises should buy AI solutions rather than building them. The exceptions are companies where AI is the core product and they can actually hire and retain top AI talent.

The solutions that actually work address the incentive problem directly.

First, share productivity gains. If AI saves twenty percent of employee time, give workers a ten percent raise. If AI drives revenue, share a percentage with users. Make efficiency a win for everyone.

Second, make AI adoption a career path. Promote AI champions. Create new roles for AI-enhanced work. Frame the change as career growth, not just job preservation.

Third, redefine middle management success. Measure managers on AI adoption metrics. Reward streamlining, not headcount growth. Make "smaller, more effective team" a win.

Fourth, prioritize transparency over fear. Be honest about automation plans. Provide transition support and training. People resist what they don't understand.

For enterprises, the action is to audit incentive alignment before any AI deployment and consider sharing ten to thirty percent of productivity gains with workers. For vendors, sell to middle managers' concerns, not just C-suite vision. For founders, build for actual workflows, not demo-able features. For everyone, recognize that adoption is a change management problem, not a technology problem.

## Chapter 5: VC Fund Economics

Most people—including many founders—don't understand how venture fund economics actually work. The math explains VC behavior: why investors need huge outcomes, why they pass on "good" deals, and why fund size determines strategy.

Consider a typical $100 million fund. The structure includes a two percent annual management fee over ten years, totaling $20 million. Carried interest is twenty percent of profits above a 1x return. The General Partners commit one to two percent of the fund themselves, around $1-2 million. The fund life spans ten years with an optional two-year extension.

This means that of the $100 million committed by LPs, only $80 million is actually investable after management fees. That capital typically gets deployed across twenty to thirty companies, with average initial checks of $2-3 million each. Forty to fifty percent is reserved for follow-on investments.

The return math is demanding. To return 3x net to LPs—a strong but achievable outcome—the fund needs to generate $300 million net for investors. Add back the $20 million in management fees already taken. Add the carry: twenty percent of the $200 million in profits equals $40 million. The fund must generate $360 million in gross returns.

From $80 million deployed, you need $360 million back—a 4.5x gross multiple.

But accounting for the inevitable failures makes the math even more demanding. Roughly fifty percent of portfolio companies fail completely, returning zero on $40 million. About thirty percent return one to two times their investment, turning $24 million into $36 million—survivals but not successes. That leaves just twenty percent as potential winners, and they must generate the bulk of returns. The $16 million invested in winners needs to return $324 million, implying an average return of twenty times or higher.

This is why VCs chase massive outcomes. A portfolio needs a few twenty to fifty times returns to make the math work.

The power law dominates venture returns. In a typical successful fund, one company might represent just five percent of capital but drive seventy-five percent or more of returns. A single hundred-times return can make an entire fund successful regardless of what happens to everything else.

Fund size dictates strategy more than anything else. A $30 million fund needs exits totaling $90 million to return 3x net; at fifteen percent ownership, that's a single $300 million exit or equivalent. A $100 million fund needs $300 million in exit proceeds, requiring $1 billion exits. A $500 million fund needs $1.5 billion in total exit value. A $5 billion fund needs $15 billion, requiring multiple $20 billion-plus outcomes. A $15 billion fund needs $45 billion, meaning they need SpaceX and Stripe-sized outcomes.

As fund size increases, the required exit size grows exponentially, but the pool of companies that can achieve those outcomes shrinks dramatically.

The key metrics in venture capital are TVPI, DPI, RVPI, and IRR.

TVPI—Total Value to Paid-In—is calculated as realized plus unrealized value divided by capital invested. It's a useful directional indicator of fund health, but unrealized value is based on marks that can be optimistic.

DPI—Distributions to Paid-In—is cash actually returned divided by capital invested. This is the only "real" metric because it represents money LPs can spend. Low DPI early in fund life is normal; low DPI late is concerning.

RVPI—Residual Value to Paid-In—measures unrealized value divided by capital invested. It represents future potential if marks are accurate, though marks can be significantly wrong.

IRR—Internal Rate of Return—is the annualized return accounting for the timing of cash flows. It's useful for comparing across vintages and strategies but can be manipulated through early small exits.

Realistic benchmarks vary by fund year. In years one and two, TVPI of 0.8x to 1.1x with zero DPI is normal—the fund is just deploying. A red flag would be TVPI above 1.5x, suggesting aggressive marking. By years three and four, TVPI should reach 1.1x to 1.5x with DPI between zero and 0.2x. TVPI below 1.0x signals problems. Years five and six should show TVPI of 1.5x to 2.0x with DPI of 0.2x to 0.5x; DPI still at zero is a red flag. By years seven and eight, TVPI should be 2.0x to 2.5x with DPI of 0.5x to 1.5x. DPI below 0.3x is concerning. In years nine and ten, funds should show TVPI of 2.5x to 3.5x with DPI of 1.5x to 2.5x, with most value realized.

Limited Partners should understand this math before committing and ask about realistic exit paths for the fund's strategy. General Partners should right-size their funds—bigger isn't better if the exit math doesn't work. Founders should know their investor's fund size and what returns they need from your company. Everyone should respect the power law; most returns come from very few companies.

## Chapter 6: Emerging Managers

Emerging managers—those managing Funds I through III, typically under $100 million—consistently outperform established funds at early-stage investing. Yet they're being systematically starved of LP capital as institutions pursue "flight to safety" into brand-name mega-funds.

This is bad for the entire ecosystem.

The performance data is clear. Emerging managers generate median net IRR of eighteen to twenty-two percent compared to twelve to fifteen percent for established funds. The top quartile of emerging managers delivers thirty-five percent plus IRR versus twenty-five percent plus for established managers. Hit rates—deals returning three times or more—run fifteen to twenty percent for emerging managers versus ten to twelve percent for established funds. Cambridge Associates, PitchBook, and Preqin data all confirm these patterns.

Why do emerging managers outperform?

First, the hustle factor. A Fund I GP's mindset is "This is my one shot to prove myself." A Fund X GP thinks "There's always the next fund." Emerging managers work harder, take more meetings, and add more value because their entire career depends on this fund working.

Second, the access advantage. Emerging managers see deals before they're "hot" because they're part of founder networks rather than sitting above them. They're not competing on valuation alone. They can write the $500,000 check that mega-funds won't bother with. And founders often want working partners who have time, not just logos.

Third, fund size math works in their favor. A $50 million fund with a $200 million exit gets a four-times contribution from that single deal. A $1 billion fund with the same $200 million exit barely moves the needle.

Fourth, ownership dynamics favor early entry. Smaller checks at earlier stages mean more ownership at lower entry prices. When the big exit happens, emerging managers own proportionally more.

Despite this performance advantage, LP capital concentration has reached troubling levels. In a single recent quarter, Andreessen Horowitz raised $7.2 billion—capturing roughly eighty percent of all venture LP commitments. The remaining thousands of funds fought for twenty percent.

This concentration happens because LP staff are evaluated on "safe" decisions. No one ever got fired for investing in a16z. It's easier to write one $100 million check than twenty $5 million checks. Brand names provide career protection. And due diligence burden is lower for known quantities.

If emerging managers disappear, the entire ecosystem suffers. Founders lose capital for the earliest stages, investors who actually have time to help, diversity of funding options, and leverage in negotiations. The industry loses the next generation of great VCs, differentiated deal sourcing, innovation in fund models, and competition that improves quality. Future returns suffer because mega-funds can't generate emerging manager returns at scale, early stages become undercapitalized, more talented founders stay at big companies, and fewer breakthrough startups get started.

When evaluating emerging managers, look for angel or syndicate track records, operating experience, and relevant domain expertise. Assess their sourcing edge: unique networks, community access, geographic advantages. Evaluate whether the fund strategy shows a clear thesis, appropriate fund size, and realistic deployment plan. Check reference quality—what do founders actually say, and would they take the money again? Understand the portfolio support model: time availability, specific skills, network access. And examine alignment through GP commitment percentage, fee structure, and incentive design.

Limited Partners should allocate twenty to thirty percent of their venture portfolio to emerging managers and build relationships with Fund I managers now—they remember who backed them. Emerging GPs should focus relentlessly on Fund I track record; everything else is noise. Founders should take meetings with emerging managers; they're hungrier and more available. Everyone should recognize that a healthy ecosystem needs diverse capital sources.

## Chapter 7: Family Offices

Family offices are the unsung heroes of early-stage venture capital. Without them, most emerging managers couldn't raise their first fund, and the pre-seed and seed market would be dramatically smaller. But family offices are not VCs—they have different goals, different timelines, and different expectations.

The numbers tell the story. In a typical emerging manager's Fund I or Fund II, family offices provide roughly sixty percent of committed capital. High-net-worth individuals contribute another eighteen percent. Fund of funds add ten percent, institutions contribute eight percent, and other sources make up the balance.

Without family offices, most first-time fund managers simply couldn't exist.

The differences between family offices and institutional VCs are significant. Family offices often find two to five times returns acceptable; institutional VCs underwrite ten times or better. Family offices operate on perpetual or multi-generational time horizons; VCs work within ten to twelve year fund cycles. Family offices have no external LP pressure—it's their own money; VCs face quarterly reporting requirements. Family offices can decide in days to weeks; institutional VCs often take weeks to months. Family offices write checks ranging from $100,000 to $10 million based on relationship and interest; VCs deploy according to defined fund strategy. Family offices follow on opportunistically; VCs reserve capital systematically. Family offices have highly variable risk tolerance; VCs are high-risk by design. Family offices prioritize long-term personal relationships; VCs evaluate deal-by-deal. Family offices deal with internal family dynamics; VCs operate within formal GP/LP structures. And critically, family offices have no exit pressure; VCs face relentless DPI demands.

Family offices come in several archetypes. Tech operating family offices—those whose wealth came from selling a technology company—are often the best fit for emerging managers and startups. They invest in a hands-on, angel-like style with typical checks of $250,000 to $2 million. They add operational expertise and network value. Decisions come quickly from the principal.

Multi-generational family offices, whose wealth came from manufacturing, retail, or real estate, are a mixed fit for venture. They invest conservatively across diversified portfolios. Checks range from $1 million to $10 million if they participate at all. They add capital and patience but often lack tech expertise. Decisions go through investment committees and move slowly.

Recently liquid family offices—those created by recent exits, IPOs, or inheritance—have high potential but are variable. They're still figuring out their investment style. Check sizes vary widely. Value-add depends on whether there's a tech background. Decisions typically involve both the principal and advisors.

Multi-family offices operate more like institutions. They're professionally managed with multiple families' capital. They invest systematically across diversified portfolios. Checks range from $2 million to $20 million. They add capital and structure. Decisions flow through investment committees.

When approaching family offices, several principles apply. Always start with relationship building. Family offices invest in people first, deals second. Build genuine connections over years, not transactional pitches. Understand their background—what industry created their wealth, and how does your opportunity connect?

Respect their uniqueness. Every family office is different. What worked with one won't necessarily work with another. Ask about their preferences: what have they invested in before, what's their typical check size, and how do they prefer to engage?

Provide appropriate access. Family offices often want more direct access than institutional LPs—meetings with founders, site visits, regular updates. This is relationship management, not reporting compliance.

Be patient with timing. Family offices don't have fundraising calendars or deployment pressures. A "no" today might become "yes" next year as their situation changes.

When taking family office capital, be clear about expectations. Discuss return expectations explicitly—they may be lower than institutional LPs expect. Clarify communication preferences: how often, what format, how much detail? Align on governance and what role they want to play. Plan for succession if this is a multi-generational relationship.

GPs should build family office relationships years before you need them and remember that every family office is different—understand their unique situation. Founders should consider family offices as strategic angels since they often have industry expertise and patient capital. Everyone should recognize that family offices are the backbone of early-stage venture, even though they rarely get credit for it.

## Chapter 8: DPI Pressure Is Destroying Value

The demand for early distributions to paid-in capital is killing bold investments and damaging long-term returns. LPs understandably want their money back, but the timing and structure of these demands is counterproductive.

The DPI timeline has accelerated dramatically. In 2019, the median first meaningful distribution came in year six of a fund's life. By 2024, that pressure had shifted to year four. Expected DPI by year five went from 0.3x to 0.5x. Earliest distribution requests moved from year three to year two. This acceleration forces behavior that destroys value.

When GPs face pressure to generate early DPI, they make decisions that look good in the short term but harm returns. They push for early exits at lower valuations rather than holding for larger outcomes. They sell winners too soon to show distributions. They avoid investments that need longer time horizons. They participate in secondary sales that lock in discounts. They prioritize small certain returns over large uncertain ones.

The math of premature distribution is brutal. Consider a company that could exit for $500 million in seven years or $200 million in four years. The seven-year exit generates $50 million at ten percent ownership, contributing 0.5x DPI to a $100 million fund. The four-year exit generates $20 million, contributing 0.2x DPI.

Choosing the early exit shows DPI sooner but sacrifices $30 million in absolute returns. Over a full portfolio, these decisions compound into massive value destruction.

Early-stage venture is structurally long-term. Company maturation takes time—building products, finding product-market fit, scaling go-to-market, achieving profitability. Exit timing is unpredictable; the IPO window opens and closes based on market conditions, not portfolio needs. Forced exits mean bad terms when LPs demand liquidity, companies get sold at whatever terms are available.

The best returns come from patient capital. Companies that defined their categories—Amazon, Google, Facebook, Stripe—took years to reach their potential. None would have generated optimal returns if VCs had been forced to exit early.

Why has this happened? The 2021 vintage created problems that reverberate today. Funds deployed unprecedented amounts of capital. Markups were aggressive based on frothy valuations. LPs expected quick returns based on fast rounds. When the market turned, that expected liquidity never materialized. Now LPs are frustrated and demanding distributions that simply aren't realistic.

At the same time, LP portfolio construction is misaligned. Many LPs need distributions from existing funds to make commitments to new funds. This creates self-referential pressure that has nothing to do with optimal investment strategy.

Career risk drives behavior too. LP investment officers face pressure to show realized returns, not unrealized marks. "We got our money back quickly" is easier to defend than "We're holding for a larger outcome."

A better approach is possible. For LPs: adjust expectations to reality. The 2021-2022 vintages will take twelve to fifteen years, not eight to ten. Evaluate GPs on decision quality, not just DPI timing. Recognize that forcing early exits destroys the value you're trying to realize.

For GPs: communicate proactively about realistic timelines. Show LPs the analysis: what does early exit cost versus holding? Build LP bases that understand venture time horizons. Don't raise from LPs who will pressure you into bad decisions.

For structuring: consider longer fund lives of twelve to fourteen years rather than ten plus two. Negotiate reasonable DPI expectations at fundraising. Include provisions for extension without LP vote if market conditions warrant. Structure carry to reward long-term holding.

The entire industry needs to recognize that DPI pressure is counterproductive. The best venture returns require patience. Forcing early liquidity destroys exactly the value everyone is trying to create.

# PART IV: EXITS AND LIQUIDITY

## Chapter 9: VC Is in the Exit Business

Most discussions about venture capital focus on investing: finding great companies, winning deals, adding value to portfolios. This emphasis is backwards. Venture capital is fundamentally in the business of exits. Everything else—sourcing, evaluation, portfolio support—exists to enable exits. Without exits, none of the rest matters.

This isn't cynical; it's structural. LPs commit capital with the expectation of receiving it back, plus returns. GPs receive carry only when profits are realized. Founders get liquidity only through exits. All incentives point toward getting companies sold or public.

Yet exit focus is often absent from VC decision-making. Investors evaluate opportunities based on team quality, market size, product differentiation, and competitive dynamics. They build portfolio support functions around operational improvement. They measure success through metrics like TVPI and markups. But they frequently don't ask the fundamental question: who will buy this company, and at what price?

Every investment decision should start with exit analysis. Before committing capital, answer these questions: Who are the likely acquirers? What have they paid for comparable companies? What metrics do they care about? What's a realistic exit timeline? What scale does the company need to achieve to be attractive?

The most common exits follow predictable patterns. Strategic M&A typically generates three to seven times revenue multiples for software companies, depending on growth and profitability. Acquirers are usually large technology companies or industry incumbents. Timeline is typically five to eight years from seed investment. Required scale is usually $30 million or more in ARR for meaningful acquisitions.

IPO outcomes generate eight to fifteen times revenue multiples for high-growth companies. Companies typically need $200 million or more in revenue with strong growth. Timeline is seven to ten years or longer from seed. Public market conditions must be favorable.

Secondary sales provide liquidity for shareholders without a company-level exit. Pricing is typically at a discount to most recent valuation. Available throughout company life but most common after Series B.

When building a portfolio, work backwards from exits. If your fund needs $500 million in exits to generate target returns, map out how you get there. How many companies need to exit at what values? What's the realistic number of IPO candidates? What's the M&A environment for your sectors?

A $100 million early-stage fund might need ten investments to have one IPO candidate, five to generate meaningful M&A outcomes, three to return capital through smaller acquisitions, and one to generate an extraordinary outcome. Build your portfolio knowing this distribution.

Exit path analysis should be ongoing, not just at investment. Annual reviews should assess each company's exit options. When the market shifts, reassess the entire portfolio. Help companies position for acquisition by building relationships with potential acquirers.

The current exit environment presents specific challenges. IPO markets remain selective after the 2022 correction, favoring profitable companies with durable growth. Tech M&A faces regulatory scrutiny, particularly for larger transactions. Private markets have reset valuations but not necessarily exit expectations.

In this environment, GPs should encourage portfolio companies to reach profitability, help build relationships with potential acquirers early, consider secondary sales for mature positions, and be realistic with LPs about extended timelines.

The bottom line is that every venture investment is a bet on a future exit. The best VCs think backwards from exit to entry, constantly evaluating whether the path to liquidity is clear. If you can't articulate how a company will exit, you shouldn't invest.

## Chapter 10: The IPO Pipeline Is Broken

The path to public markets—historically the primary way venture-backed companies returned capital to investors—is severely impaired. Companies are staying private far longer than they used to, and the IPO market has become increasingly selective.

In 2000, the median age of a company at IPO was four years. By 2024, it had reached thirteen years. The median revenue at IPO went from $20 million to over $200 million. The percentage of unprofitable companies going public dropped from eighty-five percent to forty percent. Total venture-backed IPOs fell from over 300 annually to fewer than fifty.

This shift has profound implications. VCs designed funds around seven to ten year horizons. With companies taking thirteen years to go public, the entire model is strained. More value accrues in private markets, benefiting early employees less as their equity vests long before liquidity. The pool of investors who can participate in growth-stage companies has shrunk as fewer individual investors can access private opportunities.

Several factors keep companies private longer. Access to private capital has grown dramatically—companies can raise hundreds of millions privately, eliminating the need to go public for capital. Regulatory costs of being public, particularly after Sarbanes-Oxley, make the public-company burden less attractive. Short-term market focus creates pressure for quarterly results that conflicts with long-term investment. And the success of late-stage private investors means companies don't lack for capital at any stage.

The broken pipeline creates a liquidity crisis. Companies with $50 billion private valuations can't easily go public—the market can't absorb them. Employees with significant equity can't get liquidity for years. Early investors can't distribute gains to LPs. The entire ecosystem becomes constrained.

Several dynamics must change for the pipeline to function again. Regulatory reform would help by reducing the costs and burdens of public company status. A cultural shift toward accepting lower growth rates and profitability expectations for public companies would expand the eligible pool. Better secondary markets can provide interim liquidity while companies remain private. And valuation resets have already begun, bringing private valuations closer to what public markets will accept.

The 2024-2025 IPO class will be different from the 2020-2021 class. Expect profitable or near-profitable companies. More modest valuations relative to growth. Experienced management teams who understand public market expectations. Companies with clear paths to sustainable free cash flow.

For GPs and founders, the implications are significant. Build companies that can go public—that means focusing on profitability and durable unit economics, not just growth. Develop relationships with potential acquirers as a backup path. Consider employee liquidity programs to retain talent during extended private periods. Be realistic about timeline expectations.

## Chapter 11: M&A Is Dead—Long Live Hire and License

Traditional M&A—where a large company acquires a smaller one outright—has slowed dramatically. Regulatory scrutiny, particularly for Big Tech acquirers, has created enormous uncertainty. But companies still need talent and technology. The result is the rise of "hire and license" transactions.

In a traditional acquisition, the acquiring company buys all assets, assumes liabilities, and the target company ceases to exist as an independent entity. Shareholders receive cash or stock for their equity. Employees often receive retention packages and join the acquirer.

In a hire-and-license deal, the acquiring company hires key employees—often the founding team and core engineers—and licenses the target's intellectual property. The target company may continue to exist in a diminished form or wind down. Shareholders might receive nothing, or modest payments well below what an acquisition would have generated.

This shift happened because regulatory uncertainty has made traditional acquisitions risky for buyers. The FTC and DOJ have increased scrutiny of tech acquisitions, particularly when large companies buy potential competitors. Deals that would have closed easily in 2015 now face extended reviews and potential blocks.

For acquirers, hire-and-license reduces risk. There's no deal for regulators to review. The acquiring company gets what it actually wants—the team and the technology—without the corporate overhead. Pricing is typically lower than an acquisition since there's less competition.

For shareholders, this trend is negative. Where an acquisition might have generated meaningful returns, a hire-and-license often returns very little. The acquiring company isn't buying the equity; they're hiring individuals and licensing IP.

For founders and employees, the outcomes are mixed. Key personnel get jobs, often with retention bonuses. But the equity they've accumulated may be worth far less than expected.

The most notable recent example involved Inflection AI. Microsoft hired most of Inflection's team, including founders, and licensed the technology. But Microsoft did not acquire Inflection as a company. The transaction avoided regulatory review while giving Microsoft what it wanted.

This pattern will likely continue. Until regulatory uncertainty resolves—which may require legislative action or court decisions—acquirers will prefer transactions that avoid scrutiny. Hire-and-license is the workaround.

For founders, the implications are significant. Don't assume an acquisition is the backup exit. Build companies that can survive and thrive independently. Understand that your equity may not be worth what you think if the exit is a hire-and-license rather than an acquisition. Negotiate your employment package carefully—that may be where the real value lies.

For investors, adjust your exit expectations. Traditional M&A may not be available for some portfolio companies. Evaluate companies on their ability to reach profitability and independence. Build relationships with potential acquirers early to understand their preferred transaction structures.

## Chapter 12: Secondaries Are Reshaping Liquidity

Secondary transactions—where existing shareholders sell their positions to other investors—have become an increasingly important liquidity mechanism. As traditional exits have slowed, secondaries provide an alternative path to liquidity for both investors and employees.

The secondary market has grown dramatically. Transaction volume reached $150 billion in 2023, up from $30 billion a decade earlier. Dedicated secondary funds have proliferated. Platforms for individual sellers have matured.

Secondary transactions take several forms. LP interest sales occur when a limited partner in a fund sells their position to another investor, often at a discount to NAV. Direct secondary transactions are when a shareholder in a private company sells their shares, typically to a dedicated secondary fund. Structured secondaries involve complex transactions like continuation funds or GP-led restructurings. Tender offers are when a company facilitates secondary sales for shareholders, often employees.

For sellers, secondaries provide liquidity when other options aren't available. An LP who needs capital can sell their fund position. An employee with vested shares can get cash. A VC fund can realize partial returns on a position.

For buyers, secondaries offer access to proven companies at potentially attractive prices. They can invest in later-stage companies with established metrics. Discounts to recent valuations can create value.

The pricing of secondaries depends on multiple factors. Quality of the underlying asset matters most—good companies trade near NAV, struggling ones at steep discounts. Time to expected liquidity affects pricing—the longer the wait, the larger the discount. Seller motivation drives urgency—distressed sellers accept lower prices. Information asymmetry creates risk—buyers discount for uncertainty.

Current market conditions have pushed secondary discounts wider. Average discounts reached thirty to forty percent in 2023-2024, up from ten to twenty percent pre-2022. This creates opportunity for buyers with capital and patience but means sellers are accepting significant haircuts.

For LPs considering secondary sales, timing matters. Selling at the trough locks in losses. If you can wait for market recovery, eventual pricing may be substantially better. Evaluate whether you're a forced seller or have flexibility.

For GPs, secondary transactions can be a portfolio management tool. Selling positions in later-stage companies can return capital to LPs while the companies still have upside. GP-led secondaries can reset fund timing and provide liquidity options. But alignment issues can arise—ensure LP interests are protected.

For founders and employees, company-facilitated tender offers can provide meaningful liquidity. But understand pricing—secondary buyers are sophisticated and won't pay more than they need to. Tax implications can be significant for large sales.

Secondaries are not a replacement for traditional exits, but they've become an essential part of the liquidity landscape. Understanding how they work is important for everyone in the venture ecosystem.

# PART V: BIG TECH AND INFRASTRUCTURE

## Chapter 13: Build vs. Buy—Why Enterprises Must Buy

The question of whether to build AI capabilities internally or buy them from specialized vendors has a clear answer for most enterprises: buy.

Building AI internally requires specialized talent that's extremely scarce and expensive. A competitive AI team needs machine learning engineers, data engineers, ML ops specialists, and researchers. These people command $500,000 to $1 million or more in total compensation. Recruiting and retaining them is a challenge for all but the most attractive employers.

Building also requires infrastructure investment. Training AI models requires substantial compute resources. Managing model deployment and monitoring requires specialized systems. The infrastructure investment for enterprise-scale AI runs into millions of dollars.

Building demands organizational focus. AI development isn't a side project; it requires sustained attention from leadership. Internal politics can derail initiatives. Competing priorities can starve projects of resources.

Most critically, building takes time. An enterprise starting from scratch today is years behind specialized vendors. By the time they've built, the market will have moved.

The buy case is compelling for most companies. Specialized vendors have accumulated capabilities over years. They've trained models on relevant data, built integrations, and refined their products through thousands of customer interactions. Their entire organization is focused on solving the problem.

Vendors spread development costs across many customers, making the economics work. An enterprise would bear the full cost of building. A vendor amortizes that cost across their customer base, making purchasing dramatically cheaper than building.

Vendors provide ongoing improvement. AI is not a static technology—models improve, new techniques emerge, capabilities expand. Vendors continuously improve their products. Internal teams often struggle to keep up with the pace of change.

The decision framework is straightforward. Does AI provide competitive differentiation in your core product? If no, buy. If yes, is AI your core business? If no, buy. If yes, do you have proprietary data that creates genuine advantage? If no, buy. Only if you have proprietary data that creates advantage should you consider building—and even then, be realistic about the talent, infrastructure, and focus required.

The reality is that ninety percent or more of enterprises should buy AI solutions. Building makes sense only for companies where AI is the core product and they have both the proprietary data and the organizational capability to execute.

## Chapter 14: The Billion-Dollar Engineer

When Inflection AI essentially sold its team to Microsoft for around $650 million, the per-engineer price approached $100 million. This sounds astronomical until you consider the math from the acquirer's perspective.

Microsoft has a market capitalization exceeding $3 trillion. If an AI team can improve their competitive position by even a fraction of a percent, the value creation is enormous. One percent of $3 trillion is $30 billion. Against that scale, paying $650 million for a world-class team is a rounding error.

The talent acquisition math is simple. Total cost of the team divided by expected value creation equals the return on investment. For trillion-dollar companies, paying billions for exceptional talent can generate extraordinary returns.

This dynamic explains Big Tech's willingness to pay eye-watering prices for AI companies and teams. The value created within these enormous platforms vastly exceeds the acquisition price. What looks like overpaying from the outside is actually rational investment from the inside.

For startups, this creates both opportunity and risk. The opportunity is that exceptional teams can command exceptional prices. The risk is that acquirers may prefer hire-and-license transactions that pay the team but not the equity holders.

For founders and early employees, the implications are significant. Building an exceptional team is valuable, but the value may accrue through hiring transactions rather than equity. Negotiate carefully to ensure value flows to the right places.

For investors, evaluate teams on their acquisition potential. But recognize that acquirers are increasingly sophisticated about structuring transactions to minimize what flows to shareholders.

# PART VI: PRACTICAL FRAMEWORKS

## Chapter 15: Fundraising by Stage

The mechanics of fundraising differ significantly by stage. What works for pre-seed doesn't work for Series B. Understanding these differences is crucial for founders.

At the pre-seed stage, you're typically raising $500,000 to $2 million to prove that the founding team can build something. Investors are betting on people, not metrics. The pitch focuses on founder-market fit, initial insights, and the vision. Valuations range from $5 million to $15 million pre-money. The investor base is angels, pre-seed funds, and some seed funds. Due diligence is light—investors are making conviction bets.

At seed stage, you're raising $2 million to $5 million to prove initial product-market fit. Investors want to see early traction: users, engagement, initial revenue. The pitch combines team with evidence of customer pull. Valuations range from $10 million to $25 million pre-money. Investors are seed funds, some multi-stage funds, and angels investing larger amounts. Due diligence is moderate—investors want to understand the market and the early evidence.

At Series A, you're raising $10 million to $25 million to prove scalable unit economics and a repeatable go-to-market. Investors need to see meaningful traction: typically $1 million to $3 million in ARR with strong growth. The pitch focuses on metrics, market opportunity, and the path to scale. Valuations range from $40 million to $100 million pre-money. Investors are Series A specialists and multi-stage funds. Due diligence is significant—investors will talk to customers, evaluate metrics carefully, and assess the team's ability to scale.

At Series B and beyond, you're raising $30 million or more to scale what's working. Investors expect strong metrics: $5 million or more in ARR, demonstrated unit economics, a scalable go-to-market engine. The pitch is about market leadership and the path to exit. Valuations vary widely based on metrics and market conditions. Investors are growth funds and later-stage specialists. Due diligence is extensive—investors will conduct full audits of metrics, customers, team, and market.

Several principles apply across stages. Raise when you can, not when you must. Desperation is visible and undermines negotiating position. The best time to raise is when you have momentum and don't urgently need capital.

Understand what investors at each stage want. Pre-seed investors want vision and team. Seed investors want early evidence. Series A investors want metrics and scalability. Mismatching your pitch to the stage creates friction.

Build relationships before you need them. The best fundraising comes from investors who already know you. Start building relationships twelve to eighteen months before you expect to raise.

Optimize for the right things. Higher valuations aren't always better if they come with difficult terms or misaligned investors. The best partner may not be the highest bidder.

Know your numbers cold. At Series A and beyond, investors will drill into metrics. Know your CAC, LTV, retention, growth rates, and unit economics inside and out.

## Chapter 16: The Startup Fundamentals

Before chasing AI trends or market timing, founders must nail the fundamentals. These basics determine whether a company can succeed regardless of what technologies or markets they pursue.

Product-market fit is the foundation. Without it, nothing else matters. Product-market fit means you've built something customers genuinely want and are willing to pay for. Signs of product-market fit include organic customer acquisition, strong retention, customer willingness to refer others, and measurable value creation for users.

Many founders believe they have product-market fit when they don't. Having paying customers isn't enough—they need to be retained at high rates. Growth isn't enough—it needs to be efficient. Positive feedback isn't enough—it needs to translate into business results.

Unit economics must work. The math of acquiring and serving customers must be profitable. Customer acquisition cost divided by customer lifetime value must yield a healthy ratio. For SaaS companies, a 3:1 LTV to CAC ratio or better indicates healthy economics. Payback periods should typically be eighteen months or less.

Many startups grow rapidly with broken unit economics, assuming they'll fix them later. This rarely works. Fix unit economics while small rather than hoping scale solves the problem.

Growth must be sustainable. Growth fueled by unsustainable spending isn't real growth. Evaluate what happens if you cut marketing spend by half. If growth collapses, your organic foundation is weak. Build growth engines that compound: content, community, product-led growth, network effects.

Cash management is survival. Runway is measured in months, not years. Know your burn rate and remaining cash at all times. Build eighteen to twenty-four months of runway before raising—shorter runway reduces negotiating leverage. Be willing to cut spending before it becomes critical.

Culture is strategy. Who you hire and how you work determines what you can build. Hire carefully and fire quickly when it's not working. Define and reinforce values consistently. Build systems that scale rather than relying on heroics.

Focus is everything. Startups fail more often from doing too many things than from doing too few. Choose one thing to be great at. Say no to distractions, even attractive ones. Depth beats breadth at the early stage.

## Chapter 17: Valuation Benchmarks

Valuation expectations have reset significantly from the 2021 peak. Understanding current benchmarks helps founders set realistic expectations and helps investors evaluate opportunities.

For pre-seed companies with a team and idea but no product, valuations typically range from $3 million to $8 million pre-money. Investors are betting on people and vision.

For seed companies with a product and initial users but limited revenue, valuations range from $8 million to $20 million pre-money. Some traction is expected but metrics are nascent.

For Series A companies, which should have $1 million to $3 million in ARR with strong growth, valuations range from $30 million to $80 million pre-money, representing roughly ten to thirty times ARR. Metrics matter at this stage.

For Series B companies with $5 million to $15 million in ARR and demonstrated unit economics, valuations range from $80 million to $250 million pre-money, roughly eight to twenty times ARR. Efficiency joins growth as key criteria.

These ranges vary by sector, growth rate, team quality, and market conditions. AI companies may command premiums. Companies with slower growth trade at discounts.

The most important valuation advice: don't optimize for it. A higher valuation with misaligned investors is worse than a lower valuation with great partners. Valuation matters, but it's not the only thing that matters.

Predicting the future is hazardous, but trends in motion tend to continue. Here's what I expect over the next decade.

# PART VIII: DUE DILIGENCE

## Chapter 19: LP Due Diligence on Fund Managers

Limited Partners evaluating venture fund managers need systematic approaches to assessment. The best LPs go beyond track record to understand what drives performance.

Team evaluation starts with the partners. What's their investment experience? What have they seen and done? How do they source, evaluate, and win deals? How do they work together—is this a true partnership or a collection of individuals? What happens if a partner leaves?

Track record analysis requires nuance. Raw returns matter, but attribution matters more. Which investments drove returns, and who was responsible? Were winners skill or luck? How did the manager perform in difficult vintages? What happened to the companies that didn't work?

Strategy assessment examines fit. Is the fund size appropriate for the strategy? Does the team have genuine edge in their focus areas? Is the market opportunity sufficient for the fund's return requirements? How differentiated is the approach?

Reference checks provide reality. What do founders say about working with this manager? Would they take their money again? What do co-investors say? How does the manager behave when things go wrong?

Operational due diligence covers fund operations. Are reporting and communication professional? Are valuations marked appropriately? Is the back office well-run? Are there any conflicts of interest?

The best LPs build long-term relationships with managers. They understand that a single fund is insufficient data for assessment. They evaluate process and judgment, not just outcomes. They recognize that the best managers often have unconventional approaches.

## Chapter 20: Founder Assessment

Investors evaluating founders need frameworks that go beyond pattern matching. The best founders don't always look like previous success stories.

Founder-market fit asks whether this person is uniquely suited to solve this problem. Do they have deep domain expertise or relevant experience? Do they understand the customer's pain viscerally? Do they have unfair advantages in building this particular company?

Execution ability examines whether they can actually build. What have they accomplished previously? How do they make decisions? Can they recruit and lead a team? Do they learn and adapt quickly?

Resilience matters because startups are hard. How have they handled adversity? What happened when things went wrong? Are they building for the long term or looking for quick exits?

Coachability determines whether they can improve. Do they seek and incorporate feedback? Can they be wrong and admit it? Are they building around their weaknesses?

Motivation reveals what drives them. Why are they working on this problem? What do they really want from this company? Will they persist when it gets hard?

The best assessments combine structured evaluation with genuine relationship building. Spend time with founders in different contexts. See how they handle pressure. Understand what they're really like, not just how they pitch.

## Key Metrics Reference

SaaS metrics that matter:

Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR): The annualized value of recurring subscription revenue. The most important top-line metric for SaaS companies.

Net Dollar Retention (NDR): Revenue from existing customers this year divided by revenue from those customers last year. Includes expansion, contraction, and churn. Greater than 120% is excellent; below 100% indicates net churn.

Gross Margin: Revenue minus cost of goods sold, divided by revenue. For software, should exceed 70%. Services drag reduces margins.

CAC Payback: Time to recover customer acquisition cost from gross margin. Should typically be under eighteen months for efficient businesses.

LTV:CAC Ratio: Customer lifetime value divided by customer acquisition cost. Should exceed 3:1 for healthy economics.

Logo Retention: Percentage of customers retained year over year. Should exceed 85% for SMB, 90% or more for enterprise.

## Andreessen Horowitz's $15 Billion Fundraise

In early 2024, Andreessen Horowitz raised $15 billion across multiple funds, including a $7.2 billion flagship fund. This represented a significant concentration of LP capital into a single manager.

The fundraise demonstrated the bifurcation of venture capital. LPs seeking safety concentrated in brand-name managers. a16z's platform model and brand allowed them to raise at unprecedented scale.

The math presents challenges. A $7 billion fund needs multiple $50 billion-plus outcomes to generate venture returns. The pool of such outcomes is extremely limited. The fund will likely generate private equity-like returns rather than traditional venture returns.

For the ecosystem, this concentration has implications. Capital that went to a16z didn't go to emerging managers. Founders may have fewer funding options. The diversity of the venture ecosystem could suffer.

## ServiceTitan's IPO

ServiceTitan went public in December 2024, providing a test case for the reopening IPO window. The company, which provides software for home services businesses, had reached significant scale and profitability.

The IPO priced at $71 per share, valuing the company at approximately $9 billion. This was below its peak private valuation but represented a successful outcome for investors.

Key lessons: Companies reaching profitability can access public markets. Valuations must reset from peak private levels. Execution and metrics matter more than hype.

## Figma's Journey

Figma's journey from founding through Adobe's attempted $20 billion acquisition and the deal's collapse offers lessons in value creation and exit dynamics.

Founded in 2012, Figma built a collaborative design tool that grew virally within design teams. The company achieved strong product-market fit and grew efficiently, reaching an estimated $500 million in ARR.

In September 2022, Adobe announced plans to acquire Figma for $20 billion—an extraordinary multiple. Regulatory scrutiny followed, with concerns about competition in the design software market.

In December 2023, the deal was abandoned due to regulatory challenges. Figma received a $1 billion termination fee and continues as an independent company.

The lessons: Even willing buyers and sellers can't complete deals in the current regulatory environment. Companies must be prepared to succeed independently. Termination fees provide some protection but don't replace successful exits.

## Common Founder Mistakes

After seeing thousands of pitches and working with hundreds of founders, patterns emerge. These mistakes appear repeatedly.

Raising too much at too high a valuation creates expectations that are difficult to meet. When the next round doesn't come at a higher price, down rounds damage morale and cap tables.

Scaling before product-market fit burns money and builds on a weak foundation. Growth investment should follow product-market fit confirmation, not precede it.

Hiring too fast creates culture and management challenges. Slow, careful hiring builds stronger organizations. Fast growth in headcount often outpaces the ability to manage and maintain quality.

Ignoring unit economics assumes they'll improve with scale. This rarely happens. Fix economics while small rather than hoping scale solves problems.

Building in isolation without customer feedback creates products customers don't want. Stay close to customers throughout development. Build based on their needs, not your assumptions.

Fighting with co-founders destroys companies more often than competitive or market challenges. Address interpersonal issues early and directly. Consider mediation if needed.

Waiting too long to raise forces fundraising from a position of weakness. Raise when you can, not when you must. Start the process with plenty of runway remaining.

## Building an Investment Thesis

The best investors operate from clear investment theses—developed perspectives on where value will be created. A thesis provides focus and differentiation.

Start with what you know. Your unique knowledge and experience form the foundation of your thesis. What industries have you worked in? What networks do you have access to? What trends do you see that others miss?

Define your focus. Geographic focus, sector focus, stage focus—pick your lanes. Concentration generates better returns than diversification at the early stage.

Identify your edge. Why will you see deals others won't? Why will founders choose you over alternatives? What value can you add beyond capital?

Test and refine. A thesis isn't fixed; it evolves with learning. Invest based on your thesis but update it as you see results and new information.

Communicate clearly. LPs, founders, and co-investors should understand your thesis. Clarity attracts the right opportunities.

## Term Sheet Negotiation

Term sheets establish the fundamental economics and governance of investments. Understanding key terms helps founders negotiate effectively.

Valuation and ownership are the headline terms but not the only terms that matter. A higher valuation with aggressive terms can be worse than a lower valuation with standard terms.

Liquidation preferences determine who gets paid first in an exit. 1x non-participating is standard and founder-friendly. Participating preferences or higher multiples favor investors.

Board composition affects control. Founders typically want to maintain board control, especially early. Investors may want board seats, particularly at later stages.

Protective provisions give investors veto rights over certain actions. Standard provisions are reasonable; extensive provisions can constrain operations.

Option pool expansion can effectively reduce founder ownership. Negotiate the size carefully and understand who bears the dilution.

Pro-rata rights give investors the right to maintain ownership in future rounds. This is standard and generally reasonable.

The best negotiations are not adversarial. Both sides should understand the other's interests. Reasonable terms that work for everyone create better long-term relationships than aggressive negotiation that creates resentment.

# CONCLUSION

Venture capital in 2025 and beyond will reward those who understand the fundamentals, adapt to changing conditions, and maintain focus on what matters.

For founders, this means building real companies with sustainable economics and genuine customer value. It means raising capital thoughtfully and partnering with investors who can help. It means staying focused on fundamentals even as trends shift.

For investors, this means honest assessment of opportunities and realistic expectations about returns. It means supporting founders through difficulties, not just celebrating successes. It means building lasting relationships and reputations.

For limited partners, this means understanding the asset class and its unique characteristics. It means backing emerging managers who generate outsized returns. It means patience through difficult vintages.

The venture ecosystem creates enormous value when it works well. Great companies are built. Jobs are created. Problems are solved. Wealth is generated and distributed.

This book is my attempt to share what I've learned through three companies founded, over sixty-five investments made, and thousands of hours spent thinking about this industry. I hope it's useful.

The best founders are obsessed with a problem, not just an idea. The same is true for the best investors. Find your obsession, go deep, and build something that matters.
